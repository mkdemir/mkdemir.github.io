# [](#header-1)ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange)

ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange) is a character encoding standard developed in the United States. ASCII is used for the exchange of text data between electronic devices and computers. Each character corresponds to a 7-bit number, covering a total of 128 different characters. ASCII codes range from 0 to 127.

The ASCII standard was initially established in the 1960s and revised in 1986. ASCII includes basic Latin alphabet characters (such as uppercase and lowercase letters from A to Z, numbers from 0 to 9, and common punctuation marks). It also encompasses control characters (such as CR, LF, TAB, etc.) and special characters.

ASCII forms the basis for later character encoding standards such as Unicode and is still widely used in many computer systems. However, ASCII is not sufficient for languages other than English and some Western languages, requiring additional special characters or encoding standards for non-compatible characters.

In summary, ASCII can be considered as a standard for information exchange.
